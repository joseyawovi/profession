The Price of Looking Too Young in Freelancing

  • Post On24.11.23
  • ByYawovi Joseph

As a freelancer, you're not just selling your skills—you’re selling trust, reliability, and professionalism. Early in my freelancing career, I learned this the hard way. Let me share the story of how I lost an $8,000 contract to develop a Learning Management System (LMS) simply because I looked young and inexperienced.

The Excitement of the Opportunity


I was just starting my freelancing journey with a passion for LMS development and a few edX certificates in DevOps, AWS, and cloud computing to back me up. I felt ready to tackle any challenge, and when a potential client approached me for an LMS project, I was thrilled. The contract was worth $8,000—a significant amount for someone just starting out. I had prepared thoroughly for our initial meeting, confident that my technical knowledge and enthusiasm would win them over. But as soon as I joined the virtual call, I could sense a shift in the atmosphere.

The Excitement of the Opportunity


The client’s first reaction was telling. While they were polite, I could see a hint of doubt in their eyes. I was in my early twenties, and despite my qualifications, I looked even younger. In a professional setting, appearance matters more than I realized. The client seemed hesitant to trust someone who didn’t "look the part" to manage such a significant project. Even though I answered all their technical questions confidently, providing solutions and ideas that would make their LMS efficient and scalable, the conversation never seemed to move beyond surface-level pleasantries. The client appeared unconvinced of my ability to handle a project of this scale, not because of my skills but because of my age and perceived inexperience.

Professionalism Beyond Skills


Looking back, I realize I could have done more to project professionalism. My enthusiasm was genuine, but I lacked the polished presentation that more seasoned freelancers or companies offered. I didn’t focus on creating a strong personal brand or presenting myself in a way that instilled immediate trust. In freelancing, technical skills alone aren’t enough—clients need to feel secure that they’re working with someone who understands business etiquette, professionalism, and communication.

The Cost of Inexperience

A few days later, I got the dreaded email: they had decided to go with someone else. Someone they felt was "more experienced." It wasn’t about technical expertise—I was more than capable of delivering the project. It was about confidence and trust, which I hadn’t yet mastered how to project. The $8,000 slipped through my fingers, but more importantly, I realized I needed to work on how I presented myself if I wanted to succeed.

Here’s what I took away from this experience:

  • First Impressions Are Critical:
    First Impressions Are Critical: Looking professional is as important as being professional. Invest in your personal brand—whether that’s through polished LinkedIn profiles, professional photos, or simply dressing the part for client meetings.
  • Confidence Is Key:
    Even if you’re young or just starting, present yourself confidently. Your age can be an asset, but you have to show that you’re capable of handling the responsibility that comes with a big project.
  • Build Trust Quickly:
    Clients often make quick judgments. Communicating past successes, having a strong portfolio, and being able to explain complex ideas in simple terms can help reassure them.
  • Learn From Every Experience:
    Failure isn’t final. While losing this contract stung, it was a turning point for me. I refined how I presented myself, built a more professional online presence, and approached future projects with a better understanding of how to inspire trust.

Moving Forward

Losing that contract was tough, but it forced me to grow. I’ve since learned how to balance my technical abilities with professional presence, and while I might still look young, I’ve built the confidence and reputation that now speak for me. And, believe me, the $8,000 opportunities keep coming back. If you’re a freelancer just starting out, remember this: Your skills will get you noticed, but your professionalism will win the contract. Don’t make the same mistake I did—look the part, and the opportunities will follow.
